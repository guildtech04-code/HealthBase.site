    This study presents the development of HealthBase, a web-based clinical 
information system created for Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic, a private outpatient 
facility. HealthBase is designed to support outpatient healthcare providers in 
delivering faster, safer, and more coordinated care. By addressing key 
inefficiencies in patient flow and information handling, it empowers clinics to 
transition from manual processes to intelligent, data-driven operations. The system 
aims to improve clinical operations by automating patient management, enhancing 
health data handling, and streamlining administrative tasks. Built on user-centered 
design, HealthBase enables secure access to patient records, real-time health 
monitoring, and analytics-driven decision support. A core feature is its predictive 
module, which applies machine learning to identify patients at risk of return visits 
due to recurring conditions, allowing for proactive follow-ups. Hosted on both cloud 
and local servers, it ensures data security, redundancy, and role-based access. 
Grounded in Health Information Systems and Sociotechnical Systems theories, 
HealthBase illustrates how thoughtful integration of technology with clinical 
workflows can improve efficiency, continuity of care, and decision-making in a 
modern outpatient setting.
Keywords:

Health information system, patient management, clinic operations, return visit risk 
prediction, predictive analytics, machine learning, system integration, data 
security, role-based access, outpatient care technology, Leelin Medical Clinic, ISO 
25010 Standards

Chapter 1 
INTRODUCTION

Background of the Study

    Healthcare information systems have evolved dramatically over the past few 
decades, shifting from paper-based records to integrated digital platforms that 
support various aspects of patient care [1]. In response to these advancements, 
HealthBase was developed for Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic, a modern private 
outpatient facility, to improve the efficiency and accuracy of patient management, 
appointment handling, and clinical decision-making. By moving away from manual 
paperwork, the clinic can digitally register patients, access medical histories, and 
manage consultations more effectively. HealthBase's smart scheduling 
functionality helps minimize patient wait times and allows staff to efficiently 
manage their daily workload. It also uses machine learning to assess patients who 
may be at risk of returning due to ongoing or unresolved health conditions. This 
allows clinicians to deliver proactive care that improves patient outcomes and 
reduces workflow disruptions. With visual dashboards and simplified reports, clinic 
administrators gain better oversight of operational performance, resource 
allocation, and care quality. This transition from manual to intelligent, data-driven 
tools represents a key shift in outpatient healthcare. For clinics like Dr. Leelin 
Medical Clinic, where streamlined service and personalized care are essential, a 
secure and scalable information system like HealthBase can enhance safety, 
improve coordination, and simplify internal processes while protecting sensitive 
data [2]. The system transforms data management from basic recordkeeping into


a tool that enhances clinical accuracy, operational performance, and informed care 
planning, supported by visual summaries and performance tracking. Like many 
clinics, Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic faces limitations such as scattered patient records, 
manual health calculations, and time-consuming documentation. These outdated 
processes can lead to human error and inefficiencies in care workflows. By 
introducing an automated system, HealthBase directly addresses these pain 
points and reduces the risks associated with manual data handling [3]. Recent 
studies confirm that healthcare information systems have significantly advanced 
since the COVID-19 pandemic, leading to faster digital adoption across both 
hospital and outpatient settings [4]. HealthBase reflects this progress by 
incorporating modern database architecture that supports clinic needs. Electronic 
Health Records (EHRs) are now central to patient care, enabling structured data 
entry and better care coordination [5]. This level of digital centralization is critical 
for generating useful reports and supporting timely decision-making. HealthBase 
leverages current database technologies that balance security and accessibility for 
clinical users. Its modular design allows clinics to scale system features based on 
their resources, while custom algorithms enhance the accuracy of medical 
calculations [6]. Despite these advantages, smaller healthcare facilities often face 
challenges with limited IT staffing and technical capacity. Implementing 
HealthBase in such contexts can provide a much-needed solution to these 
constraints [4]. As healthcare systems become increasingly data-driven, the ability 
to turn patient data into actionable insights through analytics and visualization tools 
becomes highly valuable [7]. Evidence shows that clinical decision support


systems, when paired with well-designed alert features, can significantly reduce 
medication errors and improve care outcomes [8]. A well-structured Database 
Management System (DBMS) includes vital components such as secure data 
storage, query handling, and standardized data entry interfaces [9]. These systems 
form the backbone of medical data operations and support compliance with 
regulatory requirements [10]. The evolution of DBMS in healthcare has shifted from 
simple file-based models to relational and even NoSQL structures, which can 
manage large volumes and varied formats of medical information [1]. Modern 
systems often integrate cloud computing and distributed architecture to boost 
scalability and performance. Improvements in data analytics have also 
strengthened big data management in clinical environments [11]. Standards like 
HL7 and FHIR now guide healthcare system interoperability, allowing HealthBase 
to exchange data efficiently with external systems [12], [13]. Implementing 
HealthBase at Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic offers transformative potential to enhance 
care quality, accuracy, and efficiency. By supporting tailored calculations and 
structured analytics, the system fills key gaps in outpatient clinical operations. 
Through data visualization and predictive tools, HealthBase helps identify patients 
likely to return for follow-up, enabling staff to take early action and improve 
continuity of care [14]. Machine learning models that evaluate demographics, 
vitals, and prior visits are shown to be effective in this type of risk prediction [15]. 
By integrating these features into the system interface, HealthBase gives clinicians 
meaningful and timely insights to guide care delivery. Predictive scoring has 
become a valuable asset in many clinical systems, supporting early detection and


case prioritization [16]. By turning raw data into clear clinical intelligence, 
HealthBase empowers staff to act faster and more confidently. It also generates 
reports that support long-term quality improvement and reduce the burden of 
manual computation in day-to-day operations [4]. This approach is especially 
helpful in clinics managing increasing patient loads and limited staff, allowing them 
to maintain performance without sacrificing care quality. The system's interface is 
built around user-centered design principles, which is a known factor in ensuring 
successful adoption of digital healthcare tools [16]. A strong user experience 
increases staff engagement and ensures the system is used consistently and 
effectively in real practice. By aligning interface design with clinical workflows and 
user habits, HealthBase offers a smooth and effective solution to modern 
outpatient data challenges. For Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic, it provides a reliable, 
intuitive platform that supports day-to-day operations, proactive care delivery, and 
long-term strategic growth..

Scope and Limitations

      This study focuses on the design and implementation of HealthBase, a 
cloud-based clinical information system specifically developed for Dr. Leelin 
Medical Clinic, a private outpatient facility. HealthBase aims to digitize core clinical 
operations, including patient registration, health record management, smart 
scheduling, and return-visit risk prediction. The system ensures accurate, 
consistent, and easily accessible patient information, streamlining daily workflows 
for healthcare professionals and supporting clinical decision-making through


organized patient data and predictive analytics. Additionally, HealthBase 
incorporates automated medical calculation features to reduce manual 
documentation tasks and improve the accuracy of patient treatment planning. 
User-friendly visualization tools are integrated to simplify the interpretation of 
clinical data. To uphold patient confidentiality and comply with privacy standards, 
the system employs appropriate access control and secure data handling 
protocols.
      However, the implementation scope of HealthBase is exclusively within Dr. 
Leelin Medical Clinic and does not extend to other healthcare providers or external 
healthcare networks. The system explicitly excludes financial management, billing 
transactions, and inventory management functionalities. While designed for cloud- 
based operations with potential future compatibility for data exchange, initial 
deployment will not include integration with external or third-party healthcare 
systems. The security measures incorporated in this study reflect standard privacy 
protection practices, and advanced cybersecurity features are not addressed 
within this research. Additionally, aspects such as comprehensive user training, 
onboarding processes, and long-term maintenance strategies fall outside the 
scope of this initial implementation.

Patient Information Management Systems and EHR Evolution

      The progressive evolution of Electronic Health Records (EHR) and Health 
Information Systems (HIS) has fundamentally transformed how healthcare 
facilities collect, organize, and utilize patient data. These systems have 
transitioned from simple repositories of patient demographics to comprehensive 
platforms that integrate medical histories, treatment plans, and clinical decisions 
across multidisciplinary teams. This shift reflects the global momentum toward 
digitizing health infrastructure to enhance patient safety, continuity of care, and 
operational efficiency. According to [53], a 25-year review of EHR systems 
illustrates how implementation has matured from basic digital archives to robust, 
interoperable systems capable of supporting population health analytics. The 
study emphasizes that successful EHR deployment is not merely a technical 
endeavor but one that relies on process alignment, leadership engagement, and 
adaptive implementation strategies. Similarly, [24] demonstrated that in Malaysian 
healthcare settings, the adoption of EHRs led to measurable improvements in 
clinical workflow, accuracy in documentation, and provider performance. These 
findings resonate with the needs of facilities like Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic, where


streamlined data access and care coordination are vital in an outpatient context. 
However, several studies highlight persistent challenges that undermine the full 
potential of EHRs. [26] and [30] note that although open-source EHRs offer cost- 
effective and customizable solutions, they frequently lack compliance with 
healthcare interoperability standards such as HL7 and FHIR. Additionally, these 
systems often underperform in environments with limited IT capacity, where 
training and support infrastructures are weak or inconsistent. [28] revealed that in 
the Philippine context, the user experience with EHR systems varies greatly 
depending on the availability of staff support, intuitive system design, and long- 
term usability planning. These challenges are compounded in resource-limited 
settings, where digital literacy remains low, and institutional resistance to 
technological change is prevalent. In addition to technical limitations, 
organizational and human factors play a decisive role. Studies like [41] emphasize 
that user satisfaction is closely tied to EHR usability, workflow alignment, and 
interface responsiveness. Without consistent training and involvement in system 
development, even the most advanced EHR systems risk underutilization or 
rejection. Meanwhile, [31] and [32] show that during health emergencies such as 
the COVID-19 pandemic, digital adoption surged but often declined post-crisis due 
to poor integration into routine operations and lack of sustainability measures. This 
suggests that successful long-term EHR deployment requires systems to be 
context-sensitive, modular, and supported by institutional change management 
strategies.


HealthBase builds on these insights by offering a localized, outpatient-focused 
solution designed for sustainable adoption in a private clinic setting. It integrates 
core EHR functionalities-such as structured patient data entry, secure health 
information access, and role-based permissions-within a flexible architecture that 
can be hosted on both cloud and local servers. In contrast to earlier systems 
criticized for lacking usability and integration ([26], [41]), HealthBase is developed 
using user-centered design principles to improve healthcare worker engagement 
and minimize resistance. Moreover, it anticipates future needs by embedding 
support for interoperability and advanced analytics, including return visit risk 
prediction.
      In conclusion, the literature demonstrates that the evolution of EHRs and 
patient information systems is marked by significant gains in care delivery but 
tempered by usability, technical, and organizational barriers. The HealthBase 
project responds directly to these challenges, offering a scalable and intuitive 
system aligned with the unique operational realities of Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. It 
reflects best practices from global research while addressing specific local needs, 
contributing to a more sustainable and effective approach to digital health 
transformation in outpatient care.



Smart Scheduling and Appointment Optimization

      In recent years, healthcare institutions have increasingly turned to digital 
scheduling solutions to mitigate inefficiencies related to long wait times, uneven


appointment distribution, and suboptimal provider-patient alignment. Studies 
across outpatient settings highlight the transformative role of intelligent scheduling 
systems in improving resource utilization, reducing patient no-shows, and 
enhancing care continuity. These systems leverage real-time availability, 
predictive algorithms, and patient-preference data to dynamically assign 
appointments, thus aligning clinical demand with service capacity. [26] reviewed 
the most recent advances in open-source EHR platforms, many of which integrate 
patient scheduling modules designed to optimize appointment timing, minimize 
overlaps, and accommodate physician availability. While the systems show 
promise, they frequently lack adaptive capabilities that factor in urgency, historical 
attendance, and patient condition severity. [33] emphasized that successful digital 
scheduling hinges on interoperability, user engagement, and system 
responsiveness to changes in care demand. These findings align with [45], who 
identified scheduling friction as a major contributor to provider burnout, particularly 
in resource-limited and high-patient-load clinics. Further, [28] explored EMR 
adoption during the COVID-19 pandemic, noting that digital scheduling saw a spike 
in use but often failed to scale sustainably post-crisis due to lack of staff training 
and poor system usability. Studies like [41] and [39] found that while clinicians 
appreciated digital tools for managing appointments, many platforms lacked 
integration with clinical decision-making systems, resulting in redundant steps and 
workflow misalignment. In high-volume outpatient environments, [36] noted that 
smart scheduling systems with built-in triage, alerts, and visit prioritization 
significantly improve throughput and patient satisfaction.


Despite these gains, limitations persist. Many platforms do not personalize 
scheduling based on contextual data such as patient demographics, travel time, or 
likelihood of reappointment. Additionally, there remains a lack of integration 
between scheduling tools and patient risk data, which could otherwise enable 
preemptive booking for at-risk individuals. These gaps hinder the ability to provide 
targeted and proactive care, especially in settings with constrained manpower or 
fluctuating demand. HealthBase addresses these shortcomings by embedding an 
intelligent, patient-centered scheduling subsystem tailored for Dr. Leelin Medical 
Clinic. It includes adaptive patient matching based on urgency, patient preference, 
doctor availability, and travel considerations. Beyond routine appointments, it 
integrates with a return visit risk prediction module that flags high-risk patients for 
follow-up prioritization. This ensures that vulnerable individuals are scheduled 
earlier, enabling proactive interventions. HealthBase also simplifies workflows 
through an intuitive dashboard that visualizes booking metrics and workload 
distribution, aiding both clinical and administrative decision-making. Unlike 
traditional schedulers, it aligns clinical urgency with system recommendations, 
ensuring that smart scheduling is not just operational but also clinically meaningful.



Return Visit Risk Prediction and Clinical Decision Support

      Return visits in outpatient settings are often linked to incomplete treatment, 
unresolved symptoms, or undetected comorbidities. Predictive analytics has 
become a crucial tool in identifying patients who may be at risk for unplanned return 
visits. A growing number of studies have explored how machine learning (ML)


models, when trained on structured EHR data, can generate risk scores that assist 
clinicians in prioritizing follow-up care. For example, [13] conducted a systematic 
review that emphasized the utility of EHR-based risk prediction models in 
accurately flagging patients with high readmission probability, showing that 
demographic variables, comorbidities, and prior visit patterns were strong 
predictors of return. Complementing this, [11] introduced a multi-output Gaussian 
process RNN capable of real-time validation for early sepsis detection, illustrating 
how temporal modeling can significantly enhance clinical decision-making. 
Although focused on inpatient contexts, the same principles apply to outpatient 
settings, especially where follow-up care is essential. Likewise, [15] highlighted the 
importance of leveraging unstructured EHR data for richer analysis, addressing 
gaps in current models that rely primarily on coded values. Their study found that 
inclusion of physician notes and patient narratives can boost predictive accuracy 
when integrated through natural language processing.
      However, the deployment of such predictive tools in smaller or private 
outpatient clinics remains limited due to infrastructure constraints, data quality 
concerns, and lack of trained personnel. [18] emphasized that data quality 
assessments are rarely automated, making it difficult to sustain reliable ML-driven 
insights over time. In the Philippine context, [20] revealed that while clinicians are 
open to predictive technology, interoperability and integration with current health 
systems remain persistent challenges. The review concluded that predictive tools 
often lack localized data sets and clinical pathways, making it harder for healthcare 
staff to trust or act on generated risk scores. Addressing these challenges, the


HealthBase system integrates a dedicated return visit risk prediction module 
designed for outpatient environments like Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. The system 
applies machine learning models to structured health data such as visit frequency, 
vitals, and diagnosis history to generate personalized return risk scores. By 
highlighting high-risk patients directly within the smart scheduling interface, 
HealthBase empowers healthcare providers to proactively intervene and prioritize 
follow-up visits. Furthermore, HealthBase features customizable clinical 
dashboards that visualize predicted risks in real time and facilitate data-driven 
planning. These innovations not only reduce the burden of manual flagging but 
also align with the broader goal of predictive, preventive, and personalized 
medicine. Through a practical and scalable implementation of clinical decision 
support, HealthBase enhances continuity of care and supports better patient 
outcomes in resource-constrained outpatient settings.








Smart Scheduling and Patient Flow Optimization in Outpatient Settings

      Efficient scheduling systems are central to outpatient service delivery, 
influencing both clinical efficiency and patient satisfaction. Traditional appointment 
systems often fail to consider variables such as urgency, provider availability, 
patient preferences, and expected consultation durations-leading to bottlenecks, 
extended waiting times, and resource underutilization. Literature over the past


decade has increasingly highlighted the potential of intelligent scheduling tools to 
transform outpatient operations. For instance, [2] emphasized that big data 
techniques can be used to dynamically adjust appointment systems in real-time, 
optimizing patient flow and reducing idle time for providers. These technologies 
enable adaptive scheduling based on patient histories, risk categories, and 
predictive metrics. Furthermore, [17] identified implementation considerations for 
deploying digital health tools in large systems, stressing that smart scheduling 
features require integration with EHRs, staff workflows, and patient communication 
channels to be effective. [8] also demonstrated the potential of analytics to 
anticipate patient demand patterns, especially when paired with historical 
appointment data, enabling proactive resource planning. Despite these benefits, 
several challenges persist. [38] found that organizational readiness, user 
resistance, and lack of training often delay the adoption of smart scheduling 
systems in developing countries. In many cases, existing hospital scheduling 
systems are static and lack decision-support mechanisms. [33] reported that users 
frequently experience frustration due to rigid appointment structures, especially 
when patient needs vary widely. The study pointed to poor interface design and 
lack of real-time flexibility as common limitations.
      The Philippine context, as discussed in [22] and [35], illustrates that even 
during the COVID-19 pandemic, many outpatient clinics continued to rely on 
manual or semi-digital scheduling methods, leading to missed appointments and 
underutilized time slots. These gaps demonstrate the need for patient-centered, 
adaptive scheduling approaches that are aligned with actual clinical workflows. In


response, the HealthBase system integrates a Smart Scheduling Module designed 
to optimize outpatient care delivery for Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. This module 
incorporates adaptive appointment allocation, which considers patient urgency, 
doctor availability, clinic capacity, and predicted return risks. The system allows 
patients to be automatically matched with the appropriate provider based on health 
concern categories and time sensitivity. Additionally, HealthBase accommodates 
dynamic adjustments, such as rescheduling based on real-time provider 
availability or patient cancellations, enhancing scheduling flexibility and reducing 
no-show rates. The integration of patient flow analytics also enables clinic 
administrators to monitor peak hours, allocate resources effectively, and improve 
the overall continuity of care. Through intelligent scheduling, HealthBase not only 
reduces wait times and overcrowding but also improves clinical efficiency and 
patient experience in a busy outpatient environment.



Health Data Visualization and Decision Support Systems

      In modern outpatient healthcare environments, the ability to transform raw 
data into actionable insights is essential for enhancing clinical decision-making, 
resource planning, and operational oversight. Decision Support Systems (DSS), 
especially those integrated with Electronic Health Records (EHRs), play a critical 
role in identifying patient risks, improving diagnostic accuracy, and ensuring timely 
interventions. Visualization tools-such as clinical dashboards and exportable 
summary reports-are now considered indispensable features of advanced Health 
Information Systems (HIS). [5] conducted a systematic review and confirmed that


Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS) can significantly improve medication 
safety, reduce adverse events, and enhance diagnostic precision when properly 
integrated with existing workflows. These systems are most effective when paired 
with clear visual indicators and alerts, which simplify data interpretation for 
clinicians. Similarly, [7] emphasized that a strong Database Management System 
(DBMS) backbone is necessary for real-time data handling and visualization, 
allowing users to view trends and metrics with minimal latency. Yet, studies such 
as [47] and [18] noted that data quality and completeness remain persistent issues. 
Poorly structured inputs, fragmented information sources, and inconsistent data 
entry protocols hinder the effectiveness of DSS and visual analytics. [54] 
underscored that even in systems with advanced alerts and dashboards, if the 
underlying data is inaccurate or delayed, decision-making can be compromised. 
Another key barrier to effective data visualization tools is the lack of training or 
standardized usage among healthcare personnel, as highlighted in [36]. Moreover, 
in resource-limited settings like many private clinics in the Philippines, 
implementation of real-time decision support is often constrained by hardware 
limitations, internet reliability, and the absence of localized predictive models. [42] 
found that healthcare workers often rely on basic reporting tools rather than 
advanced analytics, limiting the value of collected data. [51] also emphasized the 
need for dashboard customization and user-focused design in ensuring adoption 
and utility in clinical environments.
      To address these gaps, the HealthBase system developed for Dr. Leelin 
Medical Clinic integrates a robust data visualization module designed to support


predictive analytics and facilitate rapid clinical insight. The system features 
interactive dashboards that track patient demographics, visit frequencies, risk 
scores, and provider workloads. Exportable reports in various formats support both 
day-to-day management and periodic performance reviews. A key innovation is 
the predictive decision support tool embedded within the interface, which utilizes 
patient histories, vital signs, and risk factors to flag individuals at risk for early return 
visits. This feature empowers providers to plan proactive care and follow-up 
strategies. Furthermore, the system's visual indicators-such as color-coded 
alerts and severity labels-enhance usability and reduce cognitive load, making it 
easier for clinicians to prioritize patient needs. Through these tools, HealthBase 
enhances the clinic's capacity for informed, data-driven, and timely decision- 
making, while ensuring that critical information is presented in a manner that 
supports clinical efficiency and continuity of care.
Synthesis

      The collective body of related literature highlights that successful 
implementation of Electronic Health Records (EHRs) and Health Information 
Systems (HIS) requires more than just advanced technology-it demands a 
holistic strategy that integrates user-centered design, institutional readiness, 
continuous user training, and strategic policy support. While structured digital 
systems promise improved data accuracy, reduced clinical errors, and enhanced 
service coordination, these benefits are often compromised by fragmented system 
integration, limited digital literacy, and inconsistent organizational commitment. 
Case studies and longitudinal reviews stress that long-term sustainability depends


on supportive leadership, standardized data entry, stakeholder engagement, and 
a robust evaluation framework.
      Key studies also draw attention to persistent challenges in data quality, 
interoperability, and system usability. Open-source systems and proprietary 
platforms alike struggle with underdeveloped user interfaces, limited analytics 
capabilities, and inadequate customization for low-resource or outpatient contexts. 
Moreover, cybersecurity concerns, insufficient technical infrastructure, and 
resistance to workflow changes further hinder system effectiveness-particularly 
in resource-constrained healthcare environments. Addressing these issues 
requires adaptable systems that balance technical sophistication with accessibility 
and organizational fit. The evolution of clinical decision support systems (CDSS) 
and smart scheduling tools reveals a growing focus on predictive analytics, risk 
stratification, and personalized care. Literature confirms that AI-enhanced 
scheduling and return visit risk models can optimize patient flow and improve 
resource allocation. However, their practical adoption is constrained by lack of real- 
time integration, minimal validation in real-world settings, and challenges in 
aligning algorithms with actual provider routines and patient preferences. Risk 
models must also integrate both structured and unstructured health data, including 
social determinants of health, while remaining transparent, secure, and scalable. 
In addition, studies affirm that well-designed dashboards, descriptive analytics, 
and automated report generators play a vital role in turning complex patient data 
into actionable insights for clinical and administrative use. However, the success 
of these tools hinges on interoperability standards (e.g., HL7, FHIR), real-time data


accuracy, user training, and alignment with the clinical workflow. Without these 
elements, decision support tools risk underutilization or user fatigue. In direct 
response to these multidimensional challenges and insights, HealthBase was 
designed as a secure, modular, and intelligent clinical information system tailored 
for the outpatient environment of Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. Its subsystems address 
the core needs identified in the literature: patient information management, 
intelligent scheduling, real-time data visualization, and risk-aware predictive 
analytics. By embedding role-based access controls, machine learning models for 
return visit risk, adaptive appointment modules, and exportable reporting 
dashboards, HealthBase bridges the gap between academic research and 
practical system application. It aligns with evidence-based design principles by 
focusing on usability, modular scalability, and data-driven decision-making. 
Ultimately, HealthBase embodies a human-centered, analytics-supported 
approach to digital healthcare transformation. It addresses identified literature 
gaps such as weak interoperability, inconsistent EHR adoption, and limited use of 
predictive modeling in outpatient clinics. The system offers a forward-looking, 
evidence-informed solution that enhances clinical workflows, strengthens data 
governance, and supports early interventions-thus positioning itself as a 
replicable and sustainable model for other outpatient healthcare settings seeking 
to transition into intelligent, digitally mature operations.

METHODOLOGY

Research Design

      This study will utilize a quantitative research design to develop and evaluate 
HealthBase: Patient Management, Smart Scheduling, and Return Visit Risk 
Prediction tailored specifically for Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. The research will follow 
a structured two-phase approach: an initial diagnostic assessment phase and a 
subsequent system development and evaluation phase. In the first phase, a 
diagnostic assessment will be conducted through structured surveys, interviews, 
and direct observation of clinic operations. The goal is to clearly identify the 
existing challenges, workflow processes, and specific needs of healthcare 
professionals and clinic staff. This initial assessment will gather baseline 
information about patient scheduling efficiency, accuracy of patient information 
handling, current workflow challenges, and staff satisfaction levels with existing 
clinic processes. The second phase involves the design, development, and 
implementation of the HealthBase system. This phase will follow an Agile Scrum 
methodology, using iterative two-week development cycles. Each cycle will focus 
on specific features such as patient registration, patient information management, 
smart scheduling, medical data organization, and predictive analytics for 
identifying patient return visit risks. Throughout the development process, 
continuous feedback from healthcare providers and clinic staff will be actively 
gathered and integrated to ensure the final system aligns closely with real clinic 
needs and workflows.


      To evaluate the effectiveness of the implemented HealthBase system, a 
comprehensive assessment will be conducted, focusing on key performance areas 
including operational efficiency, clinical accuracy, user satisfaction, and overall 
impact on patient care. Data collection methods will include comparative analysis 
of clinic operations before and after system implementation, feedback surveys, 
system performance records, and usage logs. Standardized evaluation tools will 
measure how effectively HealthBase improves patient information management, 
scheduling efficiency, workload management, and the accuracy of predictive 
analytics. The quantitative data gathered will then be analyzed using appropriate 
statistical methods to clearly demonstrate the system's effectiveness and provide 
insights into potential areas for further improvement. Ultimately, this research 
design will provide measurable and actionable evidence demonstrating how 
HealthBase can enhance outpatient clinic operations, improve patient care 
delivery, and support clinical decision-making at Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic.
.

Software Development Methodology

      For this study, the HealthBase: Patient Management, Smart Scheduling, 
and Return Visit Risk Prediction system for Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic will be 
developed using the Agile Scrum methodology. This method allows the project 
team to work flexibly, regularly adapting to clinic requirements and ensuring the 
system closely aligns with user needs. By breaking the development process into 
smaller, manageable phases called "sprints," the team can steadily build and refine 
HealthBase, making consistent progress throughout the project. Initially, the


project team will collaborate closely with clinic staff and healthcare professionals 
to clearly define and prioritize the features required for HealthBase. This set of 
requirements, known as the product backlog, will serve as the roadmap for 
development. Each sprint will last two weeks and will focus on building specific 
modules such as patient registration, patient information management, smart 
appointment scheduling, and predictive analytics. During each sprint, the team will 
have brief daily meetings to discuss progress, identify issues promptly, and keep 
the development process running smoothly.
      At the conclusion of each two-week sprint, completed features will be 
demonstrated to clinic staff for review and feedback. These regular evaluations 
help ensure the final product matches clinic expectations and meets real-world 
needs. Feedback gathered during these reviews will directly influence subsequent 
development activities, allowing continuous refinement and improvement. Once all 
planned sprints are completed, HealthBase will undergo thorough testing to 
confirm its reliability, functionality, and ease of use. User acceptance testing (UAT) 
involving actual clinic staff will also be conducted to verify the system meets their 
operational requirements. After successful testing, HealthBase will be 
implemented for daily use in Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. Post-implementation, the 
team will provide ongoing support, collecting user feedback to continuously 
improve and enhance the system, ensuring it effectively supports clinical 
operations and patient care.

Scrum Phases

      Initiation (Project Vision & Release Planning). In this initial stage, the 
development team established the vision and clear objectives of the HealthBase 
system tailored specifically to Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. The team began by 
identifying critical clinic processes and challenges through observations, 
discussions, and structured interviews with clinic staff, doctors, and administrative

registration management, intelligent appointment scheduling, predictive analytics 
for return-visit risks, and automated medical calculations. The gathered 
information was compiled into a prioritized list known as the product backlog, 
serving as a foundation for the subsequent phases of development.


      Planning. In the planning phase, the overall vision for HealthBase was 
divided into manageable and achievable tasks scheduled into two-week 
development cycles known as "sprints." Each sprint had specific goals and 
deliverables, prioritized based on their impact on clinic efficiency and patient care. 
Initial sprints targeted the core functionalities, such as patient information 
management and smart scheduling, while later sprints addressed more advanced 
features like predictive analytics and user-interface refinements. Throughout this 
stage, the team also developed detailed system documentation, wireframes, and 
diagrams to ensure clear communication and alignment of the design with clinic 
requirements.
      Implementation. The implementation phase involved active development 
of the HealthBase system according to the defined objectives. Team members 
took responsibility for specific system components, such as patient data modules, 
predictive analytics tools, scheduling algorithms, and user-friendly interfaces. 
Regular daily meetings ("daily scrums") facilitated close collaboration, allowing the 
team to promptly address challenges, track progress, and ensure tasks were 
completed within each sprint. Continuous testing during this phase confirmed the 
reliability and functionality of each system feature before moving forward to the 
next development stage.
      Daily Scrum. Throughout each sprint, daily scrum meetings served as 
quick, focused discussions among the development team to review daily progress, 
share updates, identify immediate concerns, and realign tasks when necessary. 
These brief meetings ensured smooth coordination, clear communication, and


rapid problem resolution, maintaining steady progress aligned with project goals 
and clinic expectations.
      Review. At the conclusion of every two-week sprint, completed features 
were presented to clinic stakeholders, including doctors, healthcare professionals, 
and administrative staff. These review sessions allowed the team to demonstrate 
the developed system functionalities, gather valuable feedback, and identify 
necessary improvements. Stakeholder suggestions were then documented and 
integrated into subsequent sprints, ensuring that HealthBase continued to evolve 
in alignment with the real-world needs of Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic.
      Retrospective. Following each sprint review, a retrospective meeting was 
conducted for the development team to reflect on their collaboration, progress, and 
challenges encountered. During these discussions, the team evaluated what 
worked well, identified areas needing improvement, and defined strategies for 
enhancing efficiency and effectiveness in future sprints. Issues identified during 
retrospectives, such as unclear user requirements or technical misunderstandings, 
were promptly addressed to improve subsequent development cycles.
      Deployment. The deployment phase marked the completion of 
HealthBase's development and its transition into everyday clinical operations at 
Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. In this phase, the system was officially launched and 
made accessible to clinic staff and healthcare professionals. Final system testing 
was performed to verify usability, stability, and overall reliability. Upon deployment, 
clinic staff could start actively using HealthBase for patient management, 
scheduling, and clinical decision-making. Beyond initial deployment, the project


team remained actively engaged, providing ongoing support, gathering user 
feedback, and making continuous enhancements to ensure HealthBase 
consistently met the evolving needs of the clinic and effectively supported high- 
quality patient care.



 
Figure 3.2 Data Analysis Process

Data analysis in this study follows a structured and systematic approach to 
gathering, organizing, and interpreting clinic-related data. The aim is to generate 
useful insights that support informed decisions and improvements within Dr. Leelin 
Medical Clinic. Two main data analysis techniques were used in developing the 
HealthBase system: descriptive analytics and predictive analytics. Descriptive 
analytics provided insights into patient trends, scheduling efficiency, and frequently 
encountered medical conditions, while predictive analytics allowed the early 
identification of patients likely needing return visits.


      Step 1: Define the Question. Initially, the research team identified clear 
objectives to guide the data analysis. These objectives were based on the clinic's 
operational and clinical needs, such as understanding patient scheduling patterns, 
identifying common reasons for patient visits, and determining factors associated 
with higher return visit risks.
      Step 2: Collect the Data. Data was systematically collected from various 
sources within the clinic, including patient records, medical histories, appointment 
schedules, and clinical notes. Digital forms were utilized to ensure accurate, 
consistent data entry. Historical patient records were also reviewed, with 
appropriate permission, to support predictive analysis.
      Step 3: Clean the Data. The collected data was carefully reviewed to 
ensure accuracy and reliability. This step included removing duplicate entries, 
correcting errors, and addressing missing or incomplete data. Additionally, patient 
data was standardized, ensuring consistency inareas like medical conditions, age 
groupings, and clinic visit dates..
      Step 4: Analyze the Data. Once cleaned, the data was analyzed using 
descriptive and predictive techniques. Descriptive analytics helped identify 
patterns in patient demographics, common diagnoses, and clinic workloads, 
allowing the research team to understand existing clinic workflows clearly. 
Predictive analytics applied machine learning algorithms, such as Random Forest, 
to identify risk factors like patient age, medical history, and frequency of previous 
visits that predicted the likelihood of future clinic visits. This enabled the clinic staff 
to proactively address patient care needs.


      Step 5: Visualization. Finally, findings from the data analysis were 
presented visually using clear and easy-to-understand tools such as charts, 
graphs, and dashboards. These visualizations made it simple to spot trends and 
identify areas requiring attention. A dedicated risk prediction dashboard, featuring 
clear indicators for patients at higher risk of return visits, allowed healthcare 
providers to make informed, timely decisions. Visualization tools thus played a 
significant role in enhancing patient management and optimizing clinic resources 
effectively at Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic.

Entity Relationship Diagram


Figure 3.12. Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) of the Proposed System

      The Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) illustrates how data is structured 
and connected within the HealthBase system for Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. At the 
center is the Patient table, which stores essential patient details, including name, 
date of birth, contact information, and registration date. This table serves as the 
foundation, linking to several other key tables using the Patient_ID to ensure 
consistency across records. The Health Record table holds clinical information 
such as visit dates, chief complaints, medical history, physical examination details, 
vital signs, diagnoses, and treatment notes. This table connects directly to the 
Patient table, allowing the system to track each patient's medical journey 
accurately. The Appointment table manages scheduling information, including


appointment dates, times, types, assigned providers, and appointment status. It 
links patients and providers to maintain an organized appointment system. 
Supporting this is the Schedule Availability table, which tracks the available dates 
and times for each provider. The User Account table manages accounts for all 
system users, including doctors, clinic staff, and patients. It records details like full 
name, role, username, and account status, ensuring controlled access across the 
system.The System Log table records key health data such as blood pressure, 
heart rate, and temperature, with time-stamped entries linked to each patient. This 
allows for continuous monitoring of basic health indicators.The Risk Assessment 
table tracks patient-specific risk evaluations, including assessment dates, risk 
levels, and indicators, helping clinicians identify patients who may need closer 
follow-up. The Server table maintains information about the servers running 
HealthBase, storing details like server type, IP address, and location, supporting 
system maintenance and stability. Lastly, the Report table handles the generation 
of various reports, including who generated them, the type of report, format, and 
generation date, providing clinic staff and doctors with summarized insights for 
informed decision-making. Overall, the ERD shows how HealthBase integrates 
patient care, scheduling, clinical documentation, and system operations into a 
connected, well-organized digital ecosystem, ensuring data consistency, security, 
and usability across the clinic.




System Architecture



Figure 3.13. System Architecture of the Proposed System

The system architecture shows how HealthBase connects Dr. Leelin Medical 
Clinic's staff, doctors, and patients through a secure online platform hosted by 
Hostinger. All data is protected using SSL/TLS encryption to maintain privacy and 
security. When users interact with the system, their requests are handled by the 
web server, which runs the main system functions and manages communication 
with the database.
      The system is developed using HTML for the user interface, PHP for 
backend operations, and CodeIgniter to help organize and simplify the 
development process. All data, including patient records, health updates, and 
reports, is stored in a MySQL database, ensuring safe and well-structured data 
management.


For its prediction feature, the system uses Python in Google Colab to run a 
machine learning model that identifies patients who may be at risk of needing 
return visits. This supports improved monitoring and decision-making for the 
clinic's healthcare providers. Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic serves as the system 
administrator, with full access to all modules. Doctors can update patient details 
and manage health records, while patients can register, edit their personal 
information, and request access to their data through their own devices. All 
information is securely stored and can only be accessed according to each user's 
designated role in the system.

Research Locale

      This study has been conducted at Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic, a private 
outpatient clinic located in Makati City, Metro Manila, Philippines. The clinic serves 
a diverse range of patients, offering specialized services in Gastroenterology, 
Dermatology, and Orthopedics. With a dedicated but small team of doctors and 
administrative staff, the clinic provides daily consultations, diagnostic procedures, 
and follow-up care to both new and returning patients.


      Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic has been identified as an ideal setting for 
implementing the HealthBase system due to its growing need for more efficient 
patient data management, smart scheduling, and proactive patient monitoring. 
While the clinic has already adopted certain digital tools, such as SIRIUSMD, there 
are still gaps in areas like predictive analytics for return visit risks and streamlined 
scheduling. These conditions make the clinic a suitable research site for testing 
the effectiveness of HealthBase as a tailored system designed to improve 
outpatient workflows, enhance the quality of care, and support clinic operations 
through well-organized data management and insightful reporting tools.


Sources of Data

      The primary sources of data for this study will be gathered from the doctors, 
clinic staff, and administrative personnel of Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic. This includes 
the Gastroenterologist, Dermatologist-Orthopedic specialist, receptionists, and 
clinic assistants who are directly involved in patient care, appointment 
management, and record-keeping. Data will be collected through surveys, 
interviews, and observations to better understand the clinic's current workflows, 
challenges in managing patient information, and user needs for an enhanced 
clinical information system.
      Secondary data will come from existing clinic records, such as non- 
confidential patient profiles, appointment logs, consultation notes, and 
administrative reports. These documents will provide additional context on how the


clinic currently records, processes, and stores information, helping to inform the 
design and development of the proposed HealthBase system.


Data Collection

      Interview. The researchers will conduct interviews with key personnel at 
Dr. Leelin Medical Clinic, including doctors, administrative staff, and receptionists, 
to gather insights on the current clinic workflow, challenges in patient data 
management, and their expectations for system improvements. These semi- 
structured interviews will help identify problem areas in processes such as patient 
registration, medical record handling, appointment scheduling, and follow-up 
tracking, guiding the design of the HealthBase system.
      Observation. The researchers will also observe the clinic's day-to-day 
operations, focusing on how staff handle patient intake, update medical records, 
manage appointments, and coordinate follow-up care. By directly observing these 
processes, the researchers can pinpoint workflow inefficiencies, repetitive tasks, 
or delays that can be addressed through system automation and better information 
management.
      Survey Questionnaire. To gather user feedback and perspectives, the 
researchers will prepare and distribute survey questionnaires to clinic staff and 
doctors. These surveys will collect information about their experiences with current 
procedures, their expectations for the new system, and their evaluation of 
HealthBase in terms of usability, functionality, and efficiency, particularly during 
the system's pilot testing phase.


      System Performance Logs. The researchers will collect system 
performance data by tracking metrics from both the current processes and the 
implemented HealthBase system. This includes measuring processing times, error 
rates, system responsiveness, and reliability. This quantitative data will allow the 
researchers to compare the clinic's operations before and after HealthBase 
implementation, providing clear evidence of improvements or areas needing 
adjustment.
      Instruments. This study will use four main research instruments to collect 
well-rounded data for the development and evaluation of the HealthBase system. 
A semi-structured interview guide will gather qualitative insights from clinic 
personnel about workflow challenges, needs, and expectations for digital 
improvements. An observation checklist will help document how patient 
registration, record updates, appointment scheduling, and follow-ups are currently 
handled in the clinic. Pre- and post-implementation survey questionnaires will be 
used to assess users' knowledge, expectations, and satisfaction with the system, 
using a modified 4-point Likert scale (1: Strongly Disagree; 2: Disagree; 3: Agree; 
4: Strongly Agree).
      Additionally, a custom system performance monitoring tool will track 
quantitative metrics such as processing times, error rates, and system reliability 
before and after implementation. These combined instruments will support 
statistical analyses, such as frequency and percentage distributions for 
demographic and process-related data, arithmetic means for assessing system 
improvements, and usability scores from the modified survey tool to evaluate the


overall impact of HealthBase. Together, these instruments will ensure a thorough 
evaluation covering technical, operational, and user experience aspects 
throughout the system's development and deployment.
